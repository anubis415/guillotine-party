Meet the Candidate

David “Ninja Cat” Teague

Prospective presidential candidate under the banner of the Guillotine Party — not a career politician, donor-class insider, or polished product of the campaign machine, but someone stepping forward from lived experience and a long-standing commitment to protecting vulnerable people.

Where his politics were formed

Policy is not abstract when you have lived its consequences

David “Ninja Cat” Teague's politics were not formed in a consultant's office. They were formed in the places where policy becomes consequence — through survival, disability, neurodivergence, homelessness, study, activism, and spiritual reflection.

He has lived close enough to the edge of American failure to understand that the system is not merely broken. In many ways it is functioning exactly as designed: protecting wealth, punishing poverty, rewarding corruption, and teaching ordinary people to accept managed decline as if it were inevitable.

He has seen what happens when housing is treated as a commodity instead of a human necessity, when poverty is criminalized, and when disabled people are expected to navigate systems designed without them in mind — when children, workers, immigrants, recovering addicts, unhoused people, queer and trans people, religious minorities, Black and Indigenous communities, and poor families are treated as political talking points instead of human beings.

A simple moral position

No one is disposable

  • No human being should be treated as disposable.
  • No government should serve wealth before people.
  • No public office should be treated as private property.
  • No billionaire should have more influence over law than the citizens who must live under it.
  • No political party should be trusted more than the Constitution itself.

His loyalty is not to party machines, billionaires, corporations, dynasties, or lobbyists. His loyalty is to the people, to constitutional liberty, and to the belief that government exists to serve human life — not to manage suffering for the convenience of the powerful.

What he stands for

Anti-corruption. Pro-Constitution. Pro-people.

The Constitution is not a dead artifact, a partisan weapon, or a decorative slogan. It is a framework meant to restrain tyranny, protect dissent, secure liberty, and prevent concentrated power from ruling over the people.

The symbol is sharp because the crisis is sharp

The blade is aimed at corruption — not people.

Why the Guillotine

Severance from corruption

The name is intentionally sharp, but its meaning is symbolic and nonviolent. The guillotine does not represent harm against people. It represents severance from corruption — cutting away billionaire capture, corporate ownership of policy, authoritarian abuse, political dynasties, donor control, and the systems that keep justice broken and out of balance.

The Guillotine Party is not a revenge movement. It is an accountability movement. It is not a call for violence — it is a call to restore democratic power to the people.

Cut corruption away from government. Cut billionaire control away from democracy. Cut cruelty away from public policy. Cut the machinery of exploitation down to human size.
Developing priorities

What this campaign fights for

  • Anti-corruption and campaign finance reform
  • Constitutional accountability and democratic repair
  • Voting rights and protection of protest and civil liberties
  • Housing as a human necessity
  • Recovery and harm reduction without criminalization
  • Disability rights
  • Public education access
  • Labor dignity
  • Resistance to authoritarianism and fascism
  • Ending billionaire and corporate capture of public policy
  • Protecting children and vulnerable populations
  • Restoring public service as a moral duty
Movement-first

People before ballots

This is not a vanity project. The goal is not simply to place a name on a ballot and call that change. The goal is to help build a durable movement capable of organizing people, recruiting candidates, defending constitutional rights, challenging corruption, and creating a political home for those who no longer believe the existing system intends to represent them.

Ballot access matters. Elections matter. Legal structure matters. But none of it matters without people. The Guillotine Party must be built from the ground up — with organizers, volunteers, local chapters, state coordinators, and a platform strong enough to survive hostile interpretation.

This candidacy is not built on the belief that one person can save the country. That belief is part of the disease. It is built on the belief that people can organize, resist corruption, protect one another, and reclaim political power from those who have treated government as their private inheritance.

Are you ready to take it back?

The question isn't whether one person can fix this. It's whether enough people are ready to stop pretending the system is only broken — when the harder truth is that it has been bought.
